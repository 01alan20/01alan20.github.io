The central problem

The cliff was visible. The cost structure remained.

The demographic decline is not a surprise. Births, school cohorts and graduate projections have signaled it for years. The harder question is why so many institutions remain dependent on maintaining—or increasing—enrollment as the addressable market contracts.

01

A smaller domestic pipeline

The class of 2026 is the baseline. By 2041, the projected number of high-school graduates is materially smaller.

02

Graduates are not the same as entrants

Only a portion enrolls immediately in college, and roughly two-thirds of those entrants are assumed to choose four-year institutions in the central scenario.

03

The squeeze compounds over time

Even if college-going behavior holds steady, the four-year market contracts because the underlying cohort is smaller.

The second pressure

The international cushion is less reliable.

International enrollment remains large, but the flow of new students has weakened and policy friction has increased. That does not prove policy caused every decline. It does mean colleges should not treat international recruitment as a guaranteed replacement for shrinking domestic cohorts.

Geography matters

The national decline hides different state markets.

Some states retain or grow their local pipeline. Others become increasingly dependent on interstate, international, transfer, adult or online demand.

Local markets

The squeeze is experienced county by county.

County estimates allocate state graduate projections using current age cohorts and recent geographic movement. They are useful for recruitment-market planning, not precise school-level forecasts.

Institutional exposure

More institutions are competing for a smaller market.

Institutions with national brands can take share from weaker competitors. Smaller regional and tuition-dependent colleges are more exposed to local contraction.

The likely outcome

The squeeze will not make every college smaller by the same percentage.

It will redistribute students toward institutions with stronger brands, broader recruitment markets and deeper resources. Institutions already losing enrollment, heavily dependent on tuition and located in shrinking markets face elevated risk of downsizing, merger or closure.

Important: the current ranking measures modeled exposure, not a probability of closure. A defensible closure-risk model still requires current IPEDS balance-sheet, margin, debt, liquidity, endowment and multi-year enrollment data.
