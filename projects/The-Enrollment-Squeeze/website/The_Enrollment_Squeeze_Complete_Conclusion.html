The student pipeline has peaked.

High-school graduates are only the first stage. A smaller graduating class becomes a smaller, but still consequential, pool of likely college and four-year entrants.

By 2041, the United States is projected to produce 423,702 fewer high-school graduates and 177,390 fewer likely four-year entrants than in 2026.

How much of the pipeline remains?

The future student pool changes shape before it meets the current system.

Start with the national geography. The 2041 student pool grows in a smaller group of states while much of the Midwest and Northeast contracts.

Only after seeing where the pool changes should we ask whether each state’s resident supply can sustain its current entering classes.

Urbanization and institutional reputation appear to underpin observed growth.

Large, selective, higher-retention institutions and colleges in cities and suburbs grew more often. These are visible signals of market reach and reputation, while smaller, broadly admitting, rural, and town-based institutions declined more often.

Reputation is not measured directly. Size, selectivity, retention, and metropolitan location are treated as observable signals of institutional strength and reach. The relationships show concentration, not causation.

Growth will have to be built, not assumed.

The next phase of enrollment competition will be shaped by geography, value, and access. Urban and suburban institutions often sit closer to population growth, employers, transport, and services. Institutions in shrinking local markets will need to recruit farther from home and secure more durable student pipelines.

Students and families are also looking more closely at value. Institutions that connect academic learning to careers through employer partnerships, work-integrated learning, applied projects, and experiential teaching will be better placed to compete.

Expand reach

Build stronger domestic, international, adult, transfer, and employer-supported recruitment pipelines.

Prove value

Connect learning to careers, employers, industry projects, and real-world experience.

Remove friction

Simplify application, credit recognition, progression, and the path from interest to enrollment.

Population growth is concentrating in metropolitan America.

In 2024, 86.4% of the U.S. population lived in metropolitan areas. Metro areas accounted for 96% of national population growth between 2023 and 2024, reinforcing the advantage of institutions located near expanding labor markets and services.
