Return on Investment: Aid Spent vs Outcomes

How much enrollment gain do you buy for each dollar spent on scholarships? Breaking down ROI metrics.

ROI Interpretation:

  • Cost per additional student: ~$190,000-$200,000. This is because the $3M budget is fixed and enrollment gains are modest (~15 students).
  • The real trade-off: With a fixed budget, you choose between serving MANY students with small awards vs. FEW students with large awards.
  • Market coverage: $1k awards reach 60% of cohort; $15k awards reach only 4% of cohort.
  • Key insight: Enrollment gains are similar (~15 students) regardless of award size. The choice is about depth vs. breadth of aid.

Strategic Insights & Recommendations

Decision Framework

Choose your scholarship strategy based on institutional priorities:


Maximize Access (Social Mission):

Allocate $5,000-$7,500 per high-need student.
• Serves ~400-600 highest-need students
• Meaningful aid that reduces financial barriers
• Revenue cost: ~1.3% institutional impact
• Best for: Mission-driven, well-endowed institutions

Minimize Financial Impact:

Small scholarships ($1,000-$2,000) or no scholarships.
• Minimal revenue impact (<1%)
• Limited enrollment gain (10-15 additional students)
• Serves 1,500-3,000 students with small awards
• Best for: Revenue-constrained institutions

Strategic Middle Ground:

$3,000-$5,000 per high-need student.
• Balances access and revenue goals
• Serves 600-1,000 students meaningfully
• ~1% revenue impact (manageable)
• Best for: Most institutions

What The Model Shows

  • Realistic elasticity: 0.15 enrollment elasticity (peer-reviewed research range)
  • Modest gains: Scholarships add ~15-16 students per 1,000 cohort, not dramatically more
  • Revenue cost: Scholarships reduce net institutional revenue 1-1.5% (expected)
  • Market coverage: With $3M budget (3% of revenue), reach 200-3,000 students depending on award size
  • Why scholarships exist: Not about revenue, but about mission, equity, and access

Common Institutional Question: Don't we make money on retention?

This model shows immediate revenue impact (less enrollment × lower net tuition). In reality, students who persist and complete generate additional revenue (full-time enrollment, graduate programs, alumni giving). A more complete model would track 4-6 year cohorts, but the immediate cost is real and must be budgeted.
